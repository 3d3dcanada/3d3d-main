## Long-Form Scripts

### Long 01: The Part Was Discontinued. So I Rebuilt It.
**Target length:** 6-8 minutes

**Opening**  
One broken plastic part can stop an entire project, and the worst part is how often the original part simply does not exist anymore. No dealer stock. No clean used part. No realistic wait time. In this video I want to show how I think through a discontinued-part rebuild at 3D3D.

**Section 1: What arrives**  
Usually it is not a perfect sample. It is cracked, missing a tab, distorted, or only partly intact. Sometimes I get photos. Sometimes I get rough measurements. That is normal. The first job is not printing. The first job is figuring out what information survived.

**Section 2: Failure analysis**  
Before I model anything, I want to know what killed the original. Was it heat? UV? installation force? vibration? age? If I do not know why it failed, I am guessing twice: once in geometry and once in material.

**Section 3: Rebuild path**  
Then the process becomes measure, model, test fit, revise. If the part is fitment-sensitive, I expect at least one learning loop. The goal is not to make the prettiest file. The goal is to get back to a functional replacement.

**Section 4: Material choice**  
This is where most people think about their printer. I think about the environment. For a boat, I care about UV and moisture. For an engine bay, I care about heat and chemicals. For interior trim, I care about fit, finish, and durability.

**Close**  
If you have a broken or discontinued plastic part, send what you have. A sample, a photo, rough dimensions. Start at 3d3d.ca/quote and I will tell you if there is a practical replacement path.

### Long 02: Stop Printing Boat Parts in PLA
**Target length:** 5-7 minutes

**Opening**  
The problem with a lot of boat-part advice online is that it treats every print like it lives indoors. Boat parts do not. They live in UV, salt, moisture, heat, vibration, and repeated loading. So this video is about choosing materials for the environment, not for convenience.

**Section 1: Why marine parts fail**  
Marine failures are usually not dramatic at first. They creep, chalk, soften, crack, or lose dimensional stability until the part becomes unreliable. That is why a part that “looked fine” on the bench can still be the wrong part.

**Section 2: Material logic**  
ASA earns its place because of UV stability. PETG-CF is useful when stiffness matters. PP-based materials matter when water and chemicals are involved. TPU matters for seals and damping. If heat is a factor, the conversation changes again.

**Section 3: Geometry and mounting matter too**  
The right material does not save bad geometry. Marine parts need sane wall thickness, decent load paths, and realistic mounting assumptions.

**Close**  
If you need a boat part replaced, the right place to start is the environment it lives in. Send the part details at 3d3d.ca/marine-3d-printing.

### Long 03: Why Mechanics Make Better Fabricators
**Target length:** 6-8 minutes

**Opening**  
People often ask why I talk about mechanic work when the business is 3D printing. The answer is simple: a mechanic learns failure in the real world. Not in theory. Not in renderings. In heat, vibration, bad installs, weird tolerances, and parts that have already suffered.

**Section 1: The mechanic lens**  
When I look at a printed replacement part, I am not just asking if it matches the shape. I am asking how it gets installed, what force it sees, what touches it, what temperature it lives at, and what the failure will look like if it goes wrong.

**Section 2: Why that matters for automotive work**  
Classic-car and motorsport jobs are full of traps. The part may be small, but the environment is not gentle. That is why experience changes the quote, the design, and the material recommendation.

**Section 3: Why restraint matters**  
Some parts should not be printed. Good fabrication includes knowing when to push forward and when to stop. Trust grows when the recommendation is honest, not automatic.

**Close**  
If you have a discontinued automotive plastic part or a restoration job that needs a practical path forward, start at 3d3d.ca/automotive-3d-printing.
