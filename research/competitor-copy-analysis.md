## PART 2: PATTERN ANALYSIS

---

### HERO HEADLINE PATTERNS (What works)

**Pattern A: Capability + Outcome** (most common, 10/20)
- "Digital Manufacturing for Early Prototyping to Final Production" (Protolabs)
- "Custom Manufacturing Services Done Right" (Fictiv)
- "Financial infrastructure to grow your revenue" (Stripe)

**Pattern B: Aspirational/Emotional** (4/20 — software companies lean here)
- "Ship software peacefully" (Railway)
- "Build in a weekend, Scale to millions" (Supabase)
- "Where Big Ideas are Built" (Xometry)
- "Enabling Next Gen Hardware" (Shapeways)

**Pattern C: Three-Word Authority** (2/20)
- "Precision. Partnership. Performance." (SyBridge)
- "Make a Difference" (Materialise)

**Pattern D: Direct Service Statement** (4/20 — smaller companies)
- "3D Printing Service" (JawsTec)
- "3D printing service on demand" (MakeXYZ)
- "Your Streamlined 3D Printing Service" (Craftcloud)
- "On-Demand 3D Printing | Instant Quote" (Stratasys Direct)

**Key finding:** No successful company uses a personal/autobiographical headline. Zero out of 20 lead with the founder's story. The headline is always about what the CUSTOMER gets.

---

### HERO SUBTEXT PATTERNS (What the subtext does)

1. **Process clarity** (7/20): Explains how it works — "Upload, quote, receive"
2. **Scope definition** (6/20): Lists what they cover — "CNC, injection molding, 3D printing..."
3. **Scale reassurance** (4/20): "From one part to one million parts"
4. **Outcome promise** (3/20): "Custom mechanical parts as fast as 1 day"

**Nobody uses subtext for backstory.** Subtext is always functional — what, how fast, how much, or for whom.

---

### CTA BUTTON LANGUAGE

**Tier 1 — Action + Value (most effective pattern):**
- "Get Instant Quote" (Protolabs, Craftcloud, Sculpteo, JawsTec)
- "Get Free Pricing" (Fictiv)
- "Start Deploying" (Vercel)
- "Start your project" (Supabase)

**Tier 2 — Simple Action:**
- "Get a Quote" (Shapeways, Forecast3D)
- "Get started" (Stripe)
- "Deploy →" (Railway)

**Tier 3 — Conversational:**
- "Start the Conversation" (SyBridge)
- "Begin a Part Review" (Fathom)

**Key finding:** 15/20 use "Get" or "Start" as the first word. Nobody uses branded/clever CTA text like "Open the Quote Desk." The CTA tells you exactly what happens when you click.

---

### VOICE ANALYSIS

| Voice | Count | Companies |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| "We" + "You" | 12/20 | Protolabs, Xometry, Shapeways, Craftcloud, Fictiv, Fathom, SyBridge, Forecast3D, Materialise, Vercel, Stripe, Shop3D |
| "You" imperative only | 4/20 | Sculpteo, Supabase, Railway, MakeXYZ |
| Impersonal/third-person | 3/20 | Hubs, Stratasys Direct, marine companies |
| "I" first-person | 0/20 | NONE |

**Zero companies use "I" in their hero or primary service copy.** The closest is Fathom's customer testimonial quote. First-person singular is exclusively reserved for customer testimonials, never brand voice.

---

### STATS THAT MATTER vs. VANITY

**Stats that build trust (used by 14/20):**
- Parts manufactured: 24M+ (Shapeways, Stratasys Direct)
- Customer/company count: 5,000+ companies (Fictiv), 140K+ businesses (Craftcloud)
- Fortune 500 penetration: 61% (Fictiv), 50% (Stripe), 90% (Protolabs)
- Turnaround time: "as fast as 1 day" (Protolabs, Fictiv)
- Material/process breadth: 4,100+ combinations (Fictiv)
- Years in business: 30 years (Forecast3D), 50+ years (SyBridge)
- Certifications: ISO 9001, AS9100, ISO 13485 (Stratasys)

**Stats that DON'T appear on any of the 20:**
- Number of printers owned (only Stratasys mentions fleet size, and they have 200+)
- Personal stats ("10 years sober", "2 races")
- Social media followers
- Blog post count
- Product count under ~50

**Key finding:** Small numbers hurt more than they help. "5 Materials" or "2 Races" communicates smallness. Every competitor either shows large impressive numbers or shows NO numbers at all. There is no middle ground.

---

### SOCIAL PROOF PATTERNS

1. **Logo bars** (14/20): Enterprise customer logos, Fortune 500 names
2. **Customer outcome quotes** (8/20): Not "they were nice" but "we reduced build time by 95%"
3. **Certifications/partnerships** (6/20): ISO, AS, industry certifications
4. **User/community counts** (5/20): GitHub stars, user numbers
5. **Industry analyst recognition** (3/20): Gartner, G2, analyst mentions

**Nobody uses personal hardship as social proof.** Trust comes from outcomes, scale, and third-party validation.

---

### ABOUT/STORY PATTERNS

**How the successful 14 handle founder stories:**
- Fathom: "250+ years combined expertise" — team story, not founder story
- SyBridge: "50+ years of experience" — institutional history
- Forecast3D: "30 years in business (since 1994)" — company longevity
- Stripe: No founder story on homepage whatsoever
- Vercel: No founder story on homepage
- Railway: No founder story on homepage
- Supabase: No founder story on homepage

**When "I" is appropriate (found in about pages, not homepages):**
- Craftcloud uses first-person only in CUSTOMER testimonials
- Fathom uses "I" only in employee spotlights buried deep in the site
- No company leads with "I" on the homepage

**How to be authentic without being self-centered:**
- Lead with what the customer gets
- If origin story matters, frame it as WHY the service is better (not why the founder suffered)
- Credentials belong in a secondary "About" page, not the hero
- The story serves the trust, the trust serves the sale

## PART 3: 3D3D COPY STYLE GUIDE

Based on patterns across all 20 competitors.

---

### VOICE RULES

**Primary voice: "We" + "You"**
- "We" for capability statements: "We build parts that survive the ocean."
- "You" for benefit statements: "You get a firm quote within 24 hours."
- Reserve "I" exclusively for a clearly labeled founder section on the About page — never on the homepage hero, never in service descriptions.

**Sentence length patterns across the 20:**
- Hero headline: 3-8 words (median: 5 words)
- Hero subtext: 15-30 words (one sentence, two max)
- Service descriptions: 12-20 words per sentence
- Nobody writes multi-paragraph hero copy

**Active vs. passive:**
- 19/20 use active voice exclusively in hero and CTAs
- Passive appears only in technical specifications
- Commands are common: "Get," "Start," "Build," "Upload," "Deploy"

---

### HERO PATTERNS

**What 3D3D should do:**
1. Headline = what the customer gets or what you do (not who you are)
2. Subtext = scope + speed + differentiator (one sentence)
3. CTA = verb + clear outcome ("Get a Quote", "Start a Project")

**Recommended hero structure (based on top performers):**
```
[Eyebrow: Geographic/category positioning]
[Headline: 4-7 word capability or outcome statement]
[Subtext: 1 sentence, 15-25 words, scope + speed]
[Primary CTA: "Get a Quote" or "Start a Project"]
[Stats row: 3 proof points with REAL numbers]
```

**What the subtext should do:**
- Problem framing: "Parts that don't exist anymore? We make them." (Craftcloud pattern)
- Benefit stacking: "Marine, automotive, and field deployment. Firm quotes in 24 hours." (Protolabs pattern)
- Scale clarity: "One part or a hundred. Same operator, same quality." (Shapeways pattern)

**CTA language that converts (from the 20):**
- "Get a Quote" — universal, clear, low commitment
- "Start a Project" — implies collaboration
- "Upload Your File" — process-oriented, removes ambiguity
- AVOID: Branded clever CTAs ("Open the Quote Desk") — 0/20 competitors use these

---

### SERVICE DESCRIPTION PATTERNS

**How they frame capabilities (do this):**
- Lead with the problem or category: "Marine hardware. Automotive restoration. Prototypes."
- Follow with the differentiator: "Field-deployed, operator-run, built to survive."
- Close with the process: "Upload, quote, build, ship."

**How they use social proof (do this):**
- Customer outcomes, not testimonials about friendliness
- Specific numbers: "24M+ parts produced" not "lots of happy customers"
- Industry logos or named customers
- Certifications when applicable

**Stats that matter for 3D3D's stage:**
- Turnaround time (real, specific)
- Materials available (if the number is respectable)
- Geographic reach ("Ships worldwide from Fredericton")
- If customer count is low, DON'T show it — show capability instead

---

### ABOUT/STORY PATTERNS

**How successful companies handle founder stories:**
1. The founder story is NEVER the first thing on the site
2. It lives on a dedicated About page, usually below the mission
3. It frames hardship as the REASON the product is better, not as the emotional hook
4. The transition from "I" to "we" happens by the second paragraph
5. Credentials are shown through work output, not personal history

**Framework for 3D3D's About page:**
```
Section 1: What 3D3D does and who it serves (we/you voice)
Section 2: Why this exists — the gap in the market (impersonal)
Section 3: The operator's background — framed as credentials, not sob story
Section 4: The cooperative model — framed as innovation, not ideology
```

**When "I" is appropriate:**
- On the About page, in a clearly labeled founder section
- In blog posts and build logs
- In community/social content
- NEVER in service descriptions, hero copy, or CTAs

---

### WHAT TO NEVER DO

**Patterns that ZERO of the 20 competitors use (things 3D3D currently does):**

1. **Personal trauma in the hero section.** No competitor opens with a bomb cyclone survival story, medical diagnoses, or sobriety milestones on the homepage. This belongs on an About page, reframed as credentials.

2. **"I" voice in primary service copy.** Zero of 20 use first-person singular in their hero. "I know what breaks" reads as personal blog, not professional service.

3. **Small vanity stats.** "2 Races" and "5 Materials" signal smallness. Protolabs shows "100+ plastic materials." Shapeways shows "24M+ parts." If the number doesn't impress, don't show it.

4. **Branded/clever CTA text.** "Open the Quote Desk" is creative but adds friction. Every single competitor uses plain language: "Get a Quote," "Get Instant Quote," "Start a Project." The button should tell you what happens when you click it.

5. **Multi-paragraph hero copy.** The current 3D3D hero has 3+ sentences of body copy plus a tagline plus stats. No competitor does this. The hero is: headline, one sentence of subtext, one CTA.

6. **Printer model names as credentials.** Listing "Prusa MK2.5, Bambu A1, Creality K1, Kobra 2 Max" is like a restaurant listing their oven brands. Only Stratasys (with 200+ printers) mentions equipment, and they're the OEM. Everyone else talks about OUTCOMES, not tools.

7. **Ideology before service.** "Not a print shop. An open source cooperative." leads with what you're NOT. Every competitor leads with what you GET. The cooperative model is interesting but belongs after the value proposition, not as the value proposition.

8. **Emotional language in service sections.** "A refusal to disappear quietly" / "the weird jobs" / "give a damn" — no competitor uses emotional/defiant language in their service copy. The tone across all 20 is confident, specific, and calm.

9. **Mixing personal narrative with quote forms.** No competitor puts "Ankylosing spondylitis ended a decade of trucking" near a quote button. The path to purchase should be clean: need > capability > proof > action.

10. **No clear service categorization.** Every competitor with multiple services separates them clearly: "3D Printing | CNC | Injection Molding" (Protolabs, Fictiv, Fathom). 3D3D mixes fabrication, events, software, sponsorship, and personal story in one scroll.

---

### RECOMMENDED COPY PRIORITIES FOR 3D3D REWRITE

**Immediate (homepage hero):**
- Replace personal story with capability statement
- Reduce hero to: headline + 1 sentence + 1 CTA + 3 stats
- Change CTA to "Get a Quote" (not "Open the Quote Desk")
- Remove all "I" from homepage

**High priority (service sections):**
- Separate services into clear categories (Fabrication, Events, Network)
- Lead each with what the customer gets, not what 3D3D is
- Remove printer model names
- Remove stats under double digits

**Important (about page):**
- Keep the ocean story — but move it to About, frame as credentials
- Start About with mission/service, THEN tell the story
- Transition from "I" to "we" within the first two paragraphs
- Remove or relocate medical/sobriety details to a personal blog post

**Nice to have (trust signals):**
- Add customer outcome testimonials (even 2-3 real ones)
- Add "Shipped to X countries" if applicable
- Add certifications or material testing results if available
- Consider logo bar once enterprise/notable customers exist
